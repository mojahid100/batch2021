Stomach & Duodenum

Which parts of the duodenum are retroperitoneal?
All parts except the 1st part (intraperitoneal)
What is acute gastric dilatation associated with?
Common after splenectomy and upper abdominal surgeries
What happens in neglected cases of acute gastric dilatation?
Metabolic alkalosis
What are clinical signs of acute gastric dilatation?
Succession splash, early case: tachycardia, hiccough
What is the most common site of peptic ulcer?
Duodenum (1st part)
What is the gastroduodenal artery a branch of?
Common hepatic artery
What causes true stress ulcers leading to acute gastritis hemorrhage?
Major burns (Curling’s ulcer), head injuries (Cushing ulcer)
Where are duodenal ulcers (DU) more commonly located?
Posterior wall
What is a kissing ulcer in DU?
Ulcers on both anterior and posterior walls
What is a saddle ulcer?
Gastric ulcer (GU)
What are the clinical features of DU?
Night pain, hunger pain, usually in adults, overweight patients
What are the clinical features of GU?
Pain shortly after meals, usually in elderly, weight loss
What is seen on barium meal in DU?
Fixed-out pouching of the duodenal cap (trefoil deformity)
What is seen on barium meal in GU?
Outpouching (niche) on lesser curvature, ulcer notch on greater curvature
What is the radiological appearance of pyloric stenosis?
Soup dish stomach
What does back pain in DU or GU suggest?
Penetration into the pancreas
What are symptoms of long-standing ulcer leading to pyloric stenosis?
Projectile vomiting, pain increases with meals (eat breakfast, small lunch, can’t eat dinner)
What is the acid secretion status in Type I gastric ulcers?
Lower than normal acid secretion
What is the acid secretion status in Type II and III ulcers?
Gastric hypersecretion
What causes obstruction in DU?
Pyloric stenosis
What causes obstruction in GU?
Hourglass stomach
What is diagnostic for perforation in DU or GU?
Air under the cupula of the diaphragm
What is the standard treatment for pyloric stenosis?
Truncal vagotomy and gastrojejunostomy
What is the most common benign neoplasm in the stomach?
Leiomyoma
What is the most common malignant neoplasm in the stomach?
Adenocarcinoma
What is the safety margin for radical resection in gastric cancer?
At least 5 cm
What is the safety margin for esophageal cancer resection?
10 cm above and below
What is the most commonly performed bariatric surgery?
Sleeve gastrectomy
What type of anemia does gastric cancer lead to?
Micro or macrocytic anemia (pernicious anemia)
What are the clinical presentations of gastric cancer?
1. Discomfort after meals 2. Anorexia 3. Weight loss 4. Dysphagia 5. Ascites 6. Supraclavicular lymph node enlargement
What is the treatment for gastric cancer in the upper 1/3 of the stomach?
Total gastrectomy + Roux-en-Y esophagojejunostomy
What is the treatment for gastric cancer in the middle 1/3 of the stomach?
Subtotal gastrectomy (Billroth II) + lymphadenectomy + Roux-en-Y gastrojejunostomy
What is the treatment for gastric cancer in the lower 1/3 of the stomach?
Distal gastrectomy (partial) + lymphadenectomy + gastrojejunostomy
What are clinical signs of perforation in DU or GU?
Board-like abdominal rigidity, tenderness, air under the cupula
What are possible sites of peptic ulcer?
A. Duodenum B. Gastric antrum C. Jejunum after gastrojejunostomy D. Lower esophagus E. Small intestines F. Meckel’s diverticulum

Esophagus

What is the most common symptom of esophageal disorders?
Dysphagia
What is the most common investigation for the upper GI tract?
Endoscopy (EGD)
What is Barrett’s esophagus?
Replacement of squamous cells with columnar cells (precancerous)
When does esophageal perforation most commonly occur?
During esophagoscopy or dilatation of stricture
What is the common site of esophageal perforation?
Cricopharyngeus muscle level
What is seen on CXR in esophageal perforation?
Wide mediastinum
Should endoscopy be performed in esophageal perforation?
No, avoid endoscopy
What does a barium swallow show in corrosive esophageal injury?
Regular outline stricture
What does a barium swallow show in esophageal cancer?
Irregular narrowing (scirrhous lesions)
What are the symptoms of Zenker’s diverticulum based on size?
Small: Pharyngeal dysphagia Large: Halitosis, esophageal dysphagia
What imaging is used for the esophagus?
Barium swallow
What imaging is used for the stomach?
Barium meal
What imaging is used for the small intestine?
Barium follow-through
What imaging is used for the colon?
Barium enema
What are the characteristics of esophageal cancer?
1. More common in males 2. Old age 3. Dysphagia to solids > fluids (late: both) 4. Continuous
What are the characteristics of achalasia?
1. Equal in males and females 2. 2nd to 4th decade 3. Dysphagia to fluids and solids from the start 4. Painless 5. Intermittent
What does a barium swallow show in achalasia?
Early: Funnel shape Late: Sigmoid esophagus, hen’s beak, no gastric air bubbles
What is the most reliable treatment for achalasia?
Heller cardiomyotomy
What is the most common upper digestive disorder?
Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD)
What are the features of sliding hiatal hernia?
A. Herniation of gastroesophageal junction B. Reflux esophagitis C. Medical or surgical treatment
What are the features of paraesophageal hernia?
A. Herniation of fundus B. No reflux esophagitis C. Always surgical repair
What is the most common anti-reflux mechanism?
Nissen fundoplication (360°)
What are predisposing factors for esophageal cancer?
1. Low fiber diet 2. Alcohol 3. Smoking 4. Corrosive stricture 5. Achalasia 6. Long-standing reflux 7. Barrett’s esophagus (most important)
What is the surgical approach for lower 1/3 esophageal cancer?
Left thoracoabdominal resection (esophagogastric)
What are the surgical approaches for middle 1/3 esophageal cancer?
1. Ivor Lewis (2 incisions: midline for stomach mobilization, right 5th intercostal space) 2. McKeown (Ivor Lewis + cervical incision) 3. Blunt transhiatal total esophagectomy + neck anastomosis (no thoracotomy) 4. Thoracoscopic laparoscopic approach + cervical anastomosis
What are the advantages of gastric replacement in esophageal surgery?
A. Rich blood supply B. Complete serosal covering C. Easy mobilization

Small Intestine

What is the most common cause of bleeding per rectum in childhood?
Meckel’s diverticulum
What is the most prevalent congenital anomaly of the GIT?
Meckel’s diverticulum
What is the most common presentation of Meckel’s diverticulum?
Bleeding
What is the most common complication of Meckel’s diverticulum?
Intestinal obstruction
Where is abdominal pain located in Meckel’s diverticulum?
Around the umbilicus
What are the indications for resection of Meckel’s diverticulum?
1. Narrow-mouthed (likely diverticulitis) 2. Thickened wall (contains ectopic gastric tissue, likely peptic ulcer) 3. Attached band of adhesions (likely intestinal obstruction) 4. Young adults and children (likely various complications)
What condition mimics acute appendicitis in Meckel’s diverticulum?
Meckel’s diverticulitis
How is Meckel’s diverticulitis differentiated from acute appendicitis?
At operation, if the appendix is normal, check for Meckel’s diverticulum
What is the “rule of 2” for Meckel’s diverticulum?
A. 2% of people B. 2% of affected have complications C. 2 times more in males D. 2 feet (60 cm) from ileocecal junction E. 2 inches long
How much small intestinal secretion is produced daily?
4-6 liters
What are the functions of the terminal ileum?
A. Lymphatics (immunity) B. Enterohepatic circulation
What are the features of ulcerative-type intestinal TB?
Terminal ileum, covered with caseous material, positive Sterling sign, may lead to perforation and peritonitis
What are the features of hypertrophic-type intestinal TB?
Ileocecal, fibrosis causing intestinal obstruction, no gross caseous necrosis, narrowing of ileum with elevated cecum
What is the most common surgical complication of typhoid and paratyphoid?
Paralytic ileus
What is a common cause of small intestinal fistula?
Injury during operation
What is the common cause of colovesical fistula?
Diverticulitis of the colon (complaint: gas with urine)
Why are proximal intestinal fistulas more serious?
Higher output, more electrolyte loss (Na, K), leading to severe hyponatremia and hypokalemia
What metabolic disturbance is caused by loss of intestinal content?
Metabolic acidosis (e.g., intestinal fistula)
What metabolic disturbance is caused by loss of gastric content?
Metabolic alkalosis (e.g., pyloric stenosis)
What is required for all intestinal injuries?
Laparotomy

Disease of Peritoneum & Mesentery

E. coli
What is the mortality rate of perforated gastric or duodenal ulcer?
Nearly 20%
What is the most common cause of peritonitis in children and young adults?
Perforated appendix
What is a complication of peritonitis seen on imaging?

What is the most important diagnostic and therapeutic measure for peritonitis?
Laparoscopy
What is the most common organism in subphrenic abscess?
E. coli
What is the most common mesenteric true cyst?
Chylolymphatic cyst
What are the features of a chylolymphatic cyst?
1. Thin-walled 2. Lined by epithelium 3. Has separate blood supply
What are the features of an enterogenous cyst?
1. Thick-walled, lined with mucosa, contains mucus 2. Same blood supply as adjacent structures
What is the Tillaux triad for mesenteric cysts?
1. Cystic swelling near the umbilicus 2. Crossed by a band of resonance (bowel) 3. Mobile across, not along, the root of the mesentery
What is ascitic fluid in peritoneal tumors called?
Malignant ascites
What is pseudomyxoma peritonei?
Abdomen filled with yellow jelly-like material
What can retroperitoneal sarcoma cause?
Large bowel obstruction
What is retroperitoneal lipoma prone to?
Malignant transformation
What structure is associated with the right iliac fossa?
Appendix
What structure is associated with the left iliac fossa?
Colon

Malignant Diseases of Intestine

What are the features of Peutz-Jeghers syndrome?
Multiple polyps, melanosis (oral-lips), 3% malignant transformation
Where does non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma commonly affect the intestine?
Terminal ileum
Where does adenocarcinoma commonly affect the intestine?
Duodenum
Where does carcinoid tumor commonly affect the intestine?
Appendix
Where does familial polyposis coli (FPC) commonly affect?
Sigmoid colon and rectum
What are the features of familial polyposis coli (FPC)?
>100 polyps, autosomal dominant
Which polyps have the highest malignancy risk in FPC?
Villous adenomas >2 cm
What is the standard treatment for FPC?
Panproctocolectomy with permanent ileostomy
What is the treatment for FPC if the rectum is spared?
Ileoproctostomy + lifelong screening of rectal mucosa
What are risk factors for colorectal cancer?
Western diet, ulcerative colitis (UC), ureterocolic anastomosis, FPC, Gardner’s syndrome
What is Blumer’s shelf in colorectal cancer?
Spread to rectovesical pouch, felt by per rectal (PR) examination
What are the features of right colon cancer?
Cauliflower mass, rare intestinal obstruction, more in females, 3As (anemia, anorexia, asthenia)
What are the features of left colon cancer?
Stricture (apple core appearance), obstruction common, bleeding per rectum, more in males
What are the features of rectal cancer?
Bleeding per rectum, tenesmus, painless
What must be ruled out in middle-aged or older patients with rectal bleeding, even with hemorrhoids?
Rectal cancer
What is the surgical treatment for tumors of the cecum?
Right hemicolectomy + ileocolic and right colic vessels divided + removal of peritoneum, terminal 10 inches of ileum, cecum, ascending colon, hepatic flexure, right half of transverse colon + ileotransverse anastomosis
What is the surgical treatment for tumors of the ascending colon or hepatic flexure?
Extended right hemicolectomy + middle colic vessel divided + resection to junction of right 2/3 and left 1/3 of transverse colon + ileocolic anastomosis
What is the surgical treatment for tumors of the transverse colon?
Transverse colectomy with hepatic and splenic flexures + middle colic vessels divided + transverse mesocolon and omentum
What is the surgical treatment for tumors of the left colon?
Left hemicolectomy extending to rectum + inferior mesenteric vessels divided
What is the surgical treatment for tumors of the sigmoid colon?
Sigmoid colectomy + sigmoid vessels divided
What is the urgent treatment for colorectal cancer with intestinal obstruction in the left colon?
Colon resection and colostomy, then later anastomosis
What is the urgent treatment for colorectal cancer with intestinal obstruction in the right colon?
Resection and anastomosis immediately
What is the treatment for colorectal cancer with intestinal obstruction in critically ill patients?
Proximal colostomy only
What is the treatment for upper 1/3 rectal cancer?
Anterior restorative resection
What is the treatment for middle 1/3 rectal cancer?
Neoadjuvant chemotherapy + anterior restorative resection
What is the treatment for lower 1/3 rectal cancer?
Neoadjuvant chemotherapy + abdominoperineal resection + terminal colostomy
When is abdominoperineal resection indicated for rectal cancer?
Sphincter involvement, incontinence, concern for distal margin (<2 cm)

Intestinal Obstruction

What is simple intestinal obstruction?
Obstruction of the gut without interference with its blood supply
What is strangulated intestinal obstruction?
Impairment of blood supply; if unrelieved within 6 hours, leads to gangrene
What are the typical clinical presentations of intestinal obstruction?
Distention, vomiting, constipation, colicky pain
What is the earliest symptom of intestinal obstruction?
Pain
How does the level of obstruction affect symptoms?
Higher obstruction: earlier vomiting Lower obstruction: earlier constipation
What distinguishes paralytic ileus from other intestinal obstructions?
No colic
What are complications of paralytic ileus?
Hyponatremia, hypokalemia
What are causes of adynamic intestinal obstruction?
Mesenteric vascular occlusion (MVO), paralytic ileus
What is seen on an erect abdominal X-ray in intestinal obstruction?
Air-fluid levels
What is a characteristic feature of jejunal obstruction on X-ray?
Valvulae conniventes
What are ultrasound findings in intussusception?
Target, doughnut, pseudokidney sign
What is seen on barium enema in intussusception?
Claw sign
What are X-ray findings in volvulus?
Omega sign, coffee bean
What is seen on barium enema in volvulus?
Ace of spades, bird’s beak deformity
What does a high total leukocyte count (TLC) suggest in intestinal obstruction?
Strangulation
How does nasogastric (NG) suction affect different types of obstruction?
Simple obstruction: Relieves pain in hours Strangulated: Does not relieve pain Paralytic ileus: Relieves distention
What is the treatment for gangrenous small intestine or right colon?
Primary resection and anastomosis
What is the treatment for gangrenous left colon?
Exteriorization (Mikulicz, Hartman, or Dudley)
What is the most common cause of intestinal obstruction in newborns?
Jejunoileal atresia or stenosis
What is the most common cause of intestinal obstruction in infancy and childhood?
Intussusception, strangulated external hernia
What is the most common cause of intestinal obstruction in young and middle-aged adults?
Adhesive intestinal obstruction
What is the most common cause of intestinal obstruction in the elderly?
Malignant obstruction
In whom is volvulus common?
Elderly males with habitual constipation
When should other causes be suspected in postoperative paralytic ileus?
If symptoms persist beyond the 3rd postoperative day
What are endocrine causes of intestinal pseudo-obstruction?
Diabetes, hypothyroidism, hypoparathyroidism

Conservative treatment

Not indurated, not very painful

What is the main symptom of hemorrhoids?

When is an external hemorrhoid painful?
From the start

Staged fistulotomy (seton procedure), suture left in the remaining part

What is the most common type of anorectal abscess?
Perineal abscess
What is a horseshoe abscess?

What causes pelvirectal abscess?

What is complete rectal prolapse?

What is partial rectal prolapse?

What is the treatment for rectal prolapse in children?
Conservative treatment

Excision of excess rectal mucosa, sutures passed through rectal muscles

Narrowing of the anus

Liver

What is the anatomical position of the common bile duct in the portal triad?
Anterior and right
What is the anatomical position of the hepatic artery in the portal triad?
Anterior and left
What is the anatomical position of the portal vein in the portal triad?
Posterior
What are the boundaries of the foramen of Winslow?
Anteriorly: Porta hepatis
Posteriorly: Inferior vena cava (IVC)
Superiorly: Caudate lobe
Inferiorly: 1st part of duodenum
What is the Pringle maneuver?
Controls arterial and portal vein hemorrhage from the liver
What is Cantlie’s line?
From gallbladder fossa to inferior vena cava
What is the normal liver blood supply distribution?
75% portal vein (PV), 25% hepatic artery (HA)
What is the oxygenated blood supply distribution to the liver?
50% hepatic artery, 50% portal vein
What is the blood supply distribution in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)?
75% hepatic artery, 25% portal vein
What does the common hepatic artery divide into?
Gastroduodenal artery, hepatic artery proper
What happens if the right hepatic artery is ligated?
Jaundice, as the biliary system is only supplied by the right hepatic artery
What supplies the caudate lobe (Segment I)?
Hepatic artery and portal vein, drains directly to IVC
Which segments are drained by the right hepatic vein?
Segments VI and VII
Which segments are drained by the middle hepatic vein?
Segments IV, V, VIII
Which segments are drained by the left hepatic vein?
Segments II and III
Where does the right hepatic vein drain?
Directly into the IVC
What is the most common source of pyogenic liver abscess?
Biliary (E. coli)
What are other sources of pyogenic liver abscess and their associated organisms?
Portal vein (anaerobes, streptococci), arterial (staphylococci)
What is the treatment for pyogenic liver abscess based on source?
Biliary: Tazobactam or piperacillin
Portal vein: Metronidazole or clindamycin
Arterial: Penicillin or 3rd generation cephalosporin
What are the clinical features and treatment of pyogenic liver abscess?
Fever, abdominal pain, clear wall (ring appearance), treated with surgical intervention + systemic antibiotics
What are the features and treatment of amebic liver abscess?
Low-grade fever, shaggy wall, sterile brown chocolate pus, occurs after return from endemic areas, affects posterosuperior segment, treated with conservative medical therapy
How is echinococcal cyst (hydatid disease) diagnosed and treated?
Not aspirated for diagnosis due to spillage risk, mainly operative treatment, Casoni skin test 85% sensitive
Is hemangioma premalignant?
No, but hepatocellular adenoma is premalignant
What are the CT findings of hemangioma in different phases?
Arterial phase: Peripheral enhancement, beaded shape
Portovenous phase: Central enhancement
Delayed phase: Uniformly enhanced
What is the typical presentation of hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)?
Asymptomatic
How does HCC appear on imaging?
Enhances in arterial phase, washout in delayed phase
What is the role of alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) in HCC?
Used for prognosis and follow-up (>200 ng/mL)
What is more specific than AFP for HCC?
Des-gamma-carboxy prothrombin
In which conditions can AFP rise besides HCC?
HBV, HCV, paracetamol toxicity, acute hepatic necrosis, liver cirrhosis
What suggests HCC if alkaline phosphatase rises acutely without obstructive jaundice?
Suspicion of HCC
Why is biopsy avoided in HCC diagnosis?
Risk of bleeding and spread; use lab and imaging first
When is biopsy indicated for HCC?
In HBV or patients without cirrhosis
What are the characteristics and management of hemangioma?
Blood supply from hepatic artery, needle biopsy contraindicated, only observation
What are the features and treatment of focal nodular hyperplasia (FNH)?
Central stellate scar, stop oral contraceptives (OCPs), pregnancy not contraindicated, MRI shows cogwheel appearance, conservative treatment
What are the features and treatment of hepatocellular adenoma?
Strong association with OCPs, pregnancy contraindicated, risk of spontaneous rupture, standard treatment is resection
What is the common etiology for HCC?
Liver cirrhosis
What are the Milan criteria for HCC?
Single HCC ≤5 cm, or up to three nodules ≤3 cm, no extrahepatic spread
What is the treatment for HCC Stage 0 (single <2 cm)?
Resection (not cirrhotic)
What is the treatment for HCC Stage A?
Resection (single <5 cm, not cirrhotic), ablation (single <5 cm, cirrhotic, or 3 lesions <3 cm), liver transplantation (single <5 cm or 3 lesions <3 cm, candidate)
What is the treatment for HCC Stage B?
Chemoembolization (>3 cm and <4 cm)
What is the treatment for HCC Stage C?
Systemic agents (invasion)
What is the treatment for HCC Stage D?
Symptomatic treatment (end-stage disease)
How does fibrolamellar carcinoma (FLC) differ from HCC?
FLC: Equal in males and females, younger age, not enhanced central scar
HCC: More common in males, older age
What is a distinguishing feature of focal nodular hyperplasia (FNH)?
Enhanced central scar
What is the most common type of HCC?
Solitary HCC
What is used for HCC screening?
Ultrasound (US) and AFP
What is the most common hepatic tumor?
Metastatic liver tumor (colorectal cancer is the main source)
What is the most common benign liver tumor?
Hemangioma
What is the second most common benign hepatic tumor?
Focal nodular hyperplasia (FNH)
What is the most common malignant hepatic tumor?
Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)
What is the second most common malignant hepatic tumor?
Cholangiocarcinoma
What is the rank of HCC among cancers?
5th most common in men, 8th in women
What is the rank of gallbladder (GB) cancer among GIT cancers?
Most common biliary tract cancer, 6th most common GIT cancer

Biliary System

What are the clinical features of gallbladder pain?
Pain in right hypochondrium and epigastric regions, radiates to right shoulder and back below scapula, lasts 1-5 hours, stimulated by fatty meal
How does acute cholecystitis pain differ from gallbladder pain?
Acute cholecystitis pain lasts >6 hours
What happens in cholecystoduodenal fistula?
Stone migrates to ileum, causing intestinal obstruction; X-ray shows pneumobilia or aerobilia
What is the management of cholecystoduodenal fistula?
Surgical exploration: laparotomy, enterotomy, stone removal
What is Murphy’s sign, and how is it elicited?
Diagnostic for acute cholecystitis; press on right costal margin in midclavicular line, ask patient to take a deep breath, positive if breathing stops suddenly with tenderness
What is Charcot’s triad in ascending cholangitis?
Pain, jaundice, fever with rigors
What is Reynold’s pentad in ascending cholangitis?
Charcot’s triad + shock + altered mental status
What does Courvoisier’s law state?
In obstructive jaundice, if the gallbladder is palpable, it is most likely non-calcular (suggests malignancy, with exceptions)
What is the most common type of gallbladder stone?
Mixed
What is the treatment for asymptomatic gallbladder stones?
Conservative, no cholecystectomy (exceptions in lecture)
What is the treatment for symptomatic gallbladder stones?
Laparoscopic cholecystectomy
What are contraindications for cholecystectomy?
Bleeding tendency, cardiovascular disease, pulmonary disease
How is laparoscopic cholecystectomy performed?
Induce pneumoperitoneum with CO2 gas, use 3-4 small ports, introduce fibro-optic scope to visualize on TV screen, perform cholecystectomy with special instruments
What is the management of obstructive jaundice?
Surgical emergency: ERCP with sphincterotomy and stone removal, followed by elective cholecystectomy
What are complications of ERCP?
Perforation, bleeding, cholangitis, pancreatitis
What are the most common risk factors for cholangiocarcinoma?
Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC), choledochal cyst, flukes
What is the most common and worst type of cholangiocarcinoma?
Hilar
What does painless jaundice suggest in cholangiocarcinoma?
Malignancy
What is used for prognosis and follow-up in cholangiocarcinoma?
Biliary insulin-like growth factor
What imaging detects mass and metastasis in cholangiocarcinoma?
Dynamic CT scan
What imaging detects the vascular system in cholangiocarcinoma?
Endoscopic ultrasound (EUS)
What is the imaging of choice for cholangiocarcinoma?
Magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography (MRCP), better than ERCP, non-invasive
What imaging detects occult metastases in cholangiocarcinoma?
Positron emission tomography (PET)
What is the treatment for peripheral cholangiocarcinoma?
Hepatectomy or liver transplantation
What is the treatment for distal cholangiocarcinoma?
Whipple operation
What is the treatment for hilar cholangiocarcinoma?
Resection of caudate lobe, segment 4, right or left hepatectomy, bile duct, lymph nodes, sometimes vascular resection
Why does gallbladder cancer have a poor prognosis?
Aggressive, more common in females than males
What is the most common risk factor for gallbladder cancer?
Gallstones
When is cholecystectomy indicated for gallbladder polyps?
Polyp ≥1 cm, sessile, hyperechogenic by ultrasound
What is porcelain gallbladder?
Diffuse wall calcification seen on ultrasound
What is the treatment for gallbladder cancer stage Tis and T1a?
Cholecystectomy with R0 resection
What is the treatment for gallbladder cancer early T1b and T2?
Radical cholecystectomy
What is the treatment for gallbladder cancer late T2 and T3?
More radical cholecystectomy

Appendix

What artery supplies the appendix?
Appendicular artery, a branch of the ileocolic artery
Where is McBurney’s point located?
Lateral 1/3 and medial 2/3 of a line from the anterior superior iliac spine to the umbilicus
How is appendicitis diagnosed?
Clinical diagnosis
In which age group is appendicitis most common?
Below 40 years, rare in elderly and children
What is the most common factor in appendicitis?
Obstruction
What is hyperesthesia of Sherren’s triangle associated with?
Subhepatic appendicitis
What is the obturator sign, and when is it seen?
Pain on internal rotation, seen in pelvic appendicitis
What is the psoas sign, and when is it seen?
Pain on extension of right thigh, seen in retrocecal appendicitis
What are the cardinal features of appendicitis?
Fever (37.3-38.5°C), pain <72 hours, vomiting 1-3 times
When is vomiting more prominent in appendicitis?
If complications are present
Which groups are at higher risk for perforation in appendicitis?
Children, elderly, diabetics
Why do children have rapid generalized peritonitis in appendicitis?
Underdeveloped omentum
Why is perforation slower in the elderly with appendicitis?
Fibrotic appendix wall
What is the main symptom sequence in appendicitis?
Pain followed by vomiting
Is constipation or diarrhea more common in appendicitis?
Constipation (more common, especially in pelvic or retro-iliac appendicitis)
Why are PR and PV examinations important in appendicitis?
To exclude gynecological causes, especially in females
What is the most common position of the appendix?
Retrocecal
How does appendicitis present in pregnancy?
Pain in a higher position
Why is post-iliac appendicitis often misdiagnosed?
Mimics gastroenteritis
Who is more likely to have subhepatic appendicitis?
Young patients, no pain in shoulder or back
What are the symptoms of carcinoid syndrome?
