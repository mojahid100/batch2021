GIT Motility Disorder

What is the main risk factor for IBS?
Stress
Does IBS cause nocturnal diarrhea?
No
What are the Rome IV criteria for IBS?
A. Related to defecation B. Associated with a change in stool frequency C. Associated with a change in stool form or appearance
Where is Acute Colonic Pseudo-Obstruction (ACPO) typically seen, and what does it indicate?
Seen in the ICU; indicates a catastrophic case causing motility impairment
What is the most useful diagnostic tool for ACPO?
X-ray
What are secondary causes of esophageal dysmotility?
Diabetes Mellitus (DM), Scleroderma, Multiple Sclerosis (MS), Parkinson’s disease
What cancer is achalasia predisposed to?
Squamous cell carcinoma of the esophagus
What cancer is GERD predisposed to?
Adenocarcinoma
What are the radiological findings in achalasia?
1. Wide mediastinum + air-fluid level 2. Parrot beak narrowing
What is the radiological appearance of diffuse esophageal spasm?
Corkscrew esophagus
What is the endoscopic appearance of Barrett’s esophagus?
Salmon pink mucosa
What are the motility findings in Scleroderma?
Weak peristalsis, weak Lower Esophageal Sphincter Pressure (LESP)
What are the motility findings in achalasia?
Aprestalsis, high LESP
What are atypical symptoms of GERD?
Not specified in the document (general knowledge may include cough, hoarseness, etc.)
What is the first diagnostic tool for GERD?
Symptom response to Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPI)
When is endoscopy indicated for GERD?
Patients not responding to medical treatment for >4 weeks or with alarm signs (dysphagia, anemia, weight loss, age >55 years)
What is the gold standard diagnostic tool for GERD, and when is it used?
Ambulatory 24-hour pH monitoring; used in patients with negative endoscopy findings
What are the side effects of H2 blockers in GERD treatment?
Do not treat esophagitis (unlike PPI), tachyphylaxis
What is the most common cause of gastroparesis?
Diabetes Mellitus (DM)
What does endoscopy show in gastroparesis?
Residual food in the stomach after 8-12 hours of fasting
What are the symptoms of achalasia?
1. Dysphagia (fluids & solids) 2. Regurgitation/Chest pain 3. Heartburn 4. Aspiration 5. Weight loss
In achalasia with old age, what should be excluded?
Cancer
What is the surgical treatment for achalasia?
Heller myotomy
What is the surgical treatment for GERD?
Esophageal fundoplication
What is the cornerstone treatment for gastroparesis?
Prokinetics
What are the treatments for IBS-C?
Linaclotide, Lubiprostone
What is the treatment for IBS-D?
Rifaximin

Upper GI (UGI) Bleeding

What is the most common cause of black stool?
Iron supplementation
What is the cause of Mallory-Weiss tears?
UGI bleeding with a history of heavy alcohol use and frequent vomiting
Which medications are associated with pill esophagitis?
Vancomycin, Doxycycline
What is a common CYP450 inhibitor?
Antiviral drugs
What is a common CYP450 inducer?
Phenytoin
What are the transfusion guidelines for variceal bleeding?
A. Avoid over-transfusion B. Transfuse when Hb <7 g/dL
Where does UGI bleeding occur relative to the ligament of Treitz?
Proximal to the ligament of Treitz
Where does lower GI (LGI) bleeding occur relative to the ligament of Treitz?
Distal to the ligament of Treitz
What is the recommended intravenous access for UGI bleeding?
Two 18-gauge or larger intravenous lines
Where should patients with hemodynamic instability or active UGI bleeding be admitted?
ICU
What is a temporizing measure for uncontrollable hemorrhage due to varices?
Balloon tamponade
What is an important anatomical consideration in variceal bleeding?
Portosystemic anastomosis
What fluids should be given in hemodynamically unstable UGI bleeding?
0.9% saline or lactated Ringer’s
How many units of packed RBCs should be cross-matched in hemodynamically unstable UGI bleeding?
2-4 units
Can aggressive fluid repletion be delayed in hemodynamically stable UGI bleeding?
Yes

Lower GI (LGI) Bleeding

What is the most common cause of significant LGI bleeding?
Diverticular disease
What is the most common cause of per rectum bleeding in childhood?
Meckel’s diverticulum
Is the majority of LGI bleeding self-limiting?
Yes
What is the source of bleeding in ~90% of LGI bleeding cases?
Colon
When is colonoscopy contraindicated in LGI bleeding?
Active bleeding or hemodynamic instability
Which procedures are both diagnostic and therapeutic for LGI bleeding?
Colonoscopy, mesenteric angiography
Which procedures are diagnostic only for LGI bleeding?
Capsule endoscopy, T-99m labeled RBC scintigraphy
What is the bleeding rate detectable by mesenteric angiography?
0.5-1 mL/min
What is the bleeding rate detectable by T-99m labeled RBC scintigraphy?
0.05-0.1 mL/min
What are the contraindications for capsule endoscopy in LGI bleeding?
Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD), active bleeding
Can capsule endoscopy be controlled from outside?
No
What are common sites of diverticular disease?
Sigmoid, splenic flexure, hepatic flexure
What is the best method to diagnose diverticular disease?
Full-length colonoscopy
How many chances are given for medical treatment of diverticulitis?
One chance
What are the criteria for diagnosing massive LGI bleeding?
1. Hemodynamic instability and shock 2. Hb ≤6 g/dL or hematocrit <30% 3. Bleeding continues for 3 days 4. Significant rebleeding within 1 week 5. Transfusion of ≥4 units of blood within 24 hours
What are the indications for surgery in diverticulitis?
1. No improvement with medical therapy 2. At least 2 documented attacks 3. Complicated diverticulitis 4. Recurrent or persistent hemorrhage

Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

What are protective factors for ulcerative colitis (UC)?
Smoking, appendectomy
What are risk factors for Crohn’s disease (CD)?
Smoking, appendectomy
What layers of the bowel are affected in UC vs. CD?
UC: Mucosa + submucosa only CD: Transmural
Which is more associated with diarrhea vs. pain in UC and CD?
UC: Diarrhea > pain CD: Pain > diarrhea
What deficiencies are associated with UC and CD?
UC: Iron deficiency anemia (IDA) CD: IDA, Vitamin B12 deficiency
What is the primary location of UC vs. CD?
UC: Rectum CD: Ileum
What is a characteristic oral finding in CD?
Aphthous ulcers
What marker reflects the severity of IBD?
Fecal calprotectin (>200)
Which imaging is more important in CD than UC?
Radiological examinations
What are useful imaging studies in acute fulminant UC?
Radionuclide studies
What are endoscopic/radiological findings in UC?
Pseudopolyps, loss of haustration (lead-pipe pattern), absence of goblet cells, distorted crypts
What are endoscopic/radiological findings in CD?
Non-caseating granulomas (pathognomonic), creeping fat, cobblestone appearance, skip lesions, linear mucosal ulceration
What antibodies are associated with UC vs. CD?
UC: p-ANCA CD: ASCA
What complications are more common in CD?
Strictures (intestinal obstruction), fistulas, perineal abscesses
What complications are more common in UC?
Toxic megacolon, higher risk of malignancy than CD
What is toxic megacolon, and how is it treated?
Dilatation of the transverse colon >6 cm, associated with UC; treated by rehydration and pulse steroid therapy
What is acute colonic pseudo-obstruction (Ogilvie’s syndrome), and how is it treated?
Massive dilatation of the cecum >10 cm and right colon; treated by rehydration and neostigmine
Why does CD require antibiotics more than UC?
Extraintestinal abscesses in the abdominal cavity
What imaging is used for CD?
CT or MR enterography, capsule endoscopy (no X-ray)
What is a common extra-intestinal manifestation of UC?
Arthritis
What are the clinical features of ischemic colitis?
Common in old age (atherosclerotic) or young age; left-sided abdominal pain (sigmoid); hematochezia or bloody diarrhea with mild cramps; colonoscopy shows edema, ulcers, necrosis
What are causes of ischemic colitis?
Vasculitis (PAN), coagulation disorders, estrogen therapy, long-distance running, surgery for ileoaortic or abdominal aortic aneurysm
What is the first-line treatment to induce remission in UC?
5-ASA, sulfasalazine
What is the treatment for active proctitis in UC?
Mesalazine suppository ± 5-ASA
What is the treatment for mild/moderate acute left-sided or pancolitis in UC?
5-ASA ± prednisolone ± immunosuppressive therapy (thiopurine)
What is the treatment for severe UC?
IV fluids, IV glucocorticoids, 5-ASA, biological therapy, surgical intervention
What is the maintenance therapy for UC?
5-ASA, sulfasalazine; biological therapy in moderate/severe UC intolerant or non-responsive to immunosuppression
Can glucocorticoids be used for maintenance therapy in UC?
No
What supplements should be given with glucocorticoid therapy?
Calcium and Vitamin D
How long should glucocorticoids be used in UC?
Stopped after 8 weeks
Is surgical treatment curative in UC vs. CD?
UC: Curative CD: Not curative
Which antibiotics are used more in CD?
Cephalosporins, quinolones
What is the first-choice treatment for CD?
Budesonide
What is the most effective strategy for inducing and maintaining remission in CD?
Combination of anti-TNF and azathioprine
Is there a role for 5-ASA in CD?
No
What should be excluded before starting biological treatment in UC and CD?
UC/CD: HBV, TB CD: Extraintestinal abscesses
How long should you wait to start biological treatment after surgery?
6 months
What is the treatment protocol for toxic megacolon?
Start rehydration and pulse steroid therapy; if no improvement, refer to surgery
What defines acute severe UC?
≥6 bloody stools/24 hours plus one or more of: anemia, fever, tachycardia, high inflammatory markers
In a case with sclerosing cholangitis, abdominal pain, and diarrhea, what is the likely diagnosis?
IBD, mainly UC
What is the gold standard diagnostic tool for UC and CD?
Colonoscopy
What factors increase the risk of cancer in UC?
Autoimmune liver disease

Autoimmune Liver Disease

What is the most common clinical phenotype of Autoimmune Hepatitis (AIH)?
No symptoms or non-specific symptoms (2/3 of cases)
Where is necrosis located in true acute hepatitis (e.g., HAV)?
Zone 3 (central necrosis), no autoantibodies
Where is necrosis located in acute-on-chronic hepatitis (e.g., HBV/HCV)?
Zone 1 (periportal necrosis), autoantibodies present
What are histological findings in AIH?
Interface hepatitis, periportal necrosis, emperipolesis, rosetting
What is the initial therapy for AIH?
Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day for 2 weeks, followed by azathioprine
What is the first-line maintenance therapy for AIH?
Azathioprine
What is the second-line therapy for AIH if azathioprine fails?
Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF), calcineurin inhibitors (CNI)
What is the treatment for acute severe AIH?
High-dose IV corticosteroids; if no improvement within 1 week, emergency liver transplantation (LT)
What defines histological remission in AIH?
Hepatitis Activity Index (HAI) <4 or equivalent
How long should immunosuppressive treatment continue in AIH?
At least 3 years or 2 years
What are the initial and maintenance doses for AIH treatment?
Steroid: 0.5-1 mg/kg (initial) Azathioprine: 1-2 mg/kg (maintenance)
What bile ducts are destroyed in Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC)?
Small-to-medium bile ducts (40-80 mm)
What genetic markers are associated with PBC?
HLA DRB1*08, DQB1
What is the first reported symptom in PBC?
Fatigue
What is the cause of pruritus in PBC?
Unrelated to bile acid deposition in the skin
What is an ominous sign of disease progression in PBC?
Increased bilirubin level
What is the hallmark of PBC?
Presence of antimitochondrial antibodies (AMAs) in 90-95% of patients
What are histological findings in PBC?
Epithelioid aggregates or granulomas; fibrosis and cirrhosis develop later
What is the major medication to slow PBC progression?
Ursodeoxycholic acid
What is the first-line treatment for pruritus in PBC?
Antihistamines (caution if signs of encephalopathy)
What is the treatment for PBC if it progresses to cirrhosis?
Liver transplantation
What condition is Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC) strongly associated with?
IBD (mainly UC, with rectal sparing and backwash ileitis)
What malignancy is PSC often complicated by?
Cholangiocarcinoma
Does the course of IBD correlate with PSC?
No
What may early hypoalbuminemia indicate in PSC?
Active IBD
What is the most common characteristic feature of PSC?
Onion skin fibrosis
What surveillance is recommended for PSC patients?
Colonoscopy and biopsy (for IBD/colorectal carcinoma), CA 19-9 (cholangiocarcinoma), US (gallbladder cancer)

Clinical Nutrition

What conditions are associated with time-limited, intense inflammation?
Acute trauma, pneumonia, closed head injury, burn injury
What conditions are associated with low-intensity, sustained inflammation?
Cardiac or cancer cachexia, obesity
Can nutrition supplementation reverse malnutrition with inflammation?
No, treat the underlying cause
In what conditions is NPO (nil per os) indicated?
Coma, trauma, bulbar syndrome
What is the maximum duration for a nasogastric tube, and why?
4 days, due to risk of esophageal ulcer, esophagitis, pneumonia
After 4 days of nasogastric tube use, what is the next step?
Endoscopic enterostomy
What length of small intestine + colon supports enteral nutrition?
≥60-90 cm
What length of small intestine alone supports enteral nutrition?
≥150 cm
If the intestine is shorter than the thresholds, what nutrition is used?
Parenteral nutrition (PN)
What are the clinical features of riboflavin deficiency?
Moon face, angular stomatitis, magenta tongue
What is caused by thiamine deficiency?
Wernicke’s encephalopathy
What are the clinical features of zinc deficiency?
Goose flesh, seborrheic rash, reduced taste
What is caused by niacin deficiency?
Pellagra
What are the clinical features of vitamin C deficiency?
Scurvy, perifollicular hemorrhage, corkscrew hair
What is caused by vitamin K deficiency?
Bruising
What are the clinical features of vitamin A deficiency?
Perifollicular hyperkeratosis, Bitot’s spots
What is the clinical feature of iron deficiency affecting nails?
Koilonychia
What are the clinical features of selenium deficiency?
Cardiomyopathy, myalgias
Where are amino acids and iron absorbed?
Duodenum
Where are calcium, folate, water, and sodium absorbed?
Jejunum
Where are vitamin B12, intrinsic factor, and bile acids absorbed?
Ileum
What does the colon absorb?
Small amounts of water, electrolytes, and bile acids

Constipation & Diarrhea

What are the Rome IV criteria for constipation (2 or more for 3 months)?
1. <3 spontaneous bowel movements/week 2. Straining 3. Lumpy stool 4. Sensation of anorectal obstruction/blockage 5. Sensation of incomplete defecation 6. Manual maneuvering required to defecate
What are alarming features in constipation?
Bleeding, weight loss, new onset >50 years, anemia, obstructive symptoms
What are clinical features of pelvic floor dysfunction (defecatory disorders, dyssynergia)?
Prolonged/excessive straining, sense of incomplete evacuation
What is the specificity and sensitivity of the balloon expulsion test for dyssynergia?
High specificity, low sensitivity
What does the wireless motility capsule measure?
pH, temperature, pressure
What is used in defecatory disorders to aid defecation?
Glycerine suppository
What is the treatment for pelvic floor dysfunction alone?
Biofeedback training
What is used in elderly or neurologic disorder-related constipation?
Hypertonic phosphate enema
What stimulates motility and secretion in constipation?
Phenolphthalein
How is diarrhea defined?
Increased stool weight >200 g/day
What does high stool Na-K indicate in diarrhea?
Low osmotic gap
What characterizes osmotic diarrhea?
High osmotic gap (>150), stops with fasting, caused by lactose intolerance, gastrocolic fistula, Mg sulfate ingestion
What characterizes secretory diarrhea?
Low osmotic gap (<50), persists with fasting, caused by enterotoxigenic bacteria, Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, carcinoid syndrome, intact intestinal mucosa
What causes exudate diarrhea?
Mucosal destruction (e.g., IBD, enteroinvasive bacteria)
What do long-term antibiotic use lead to?
Pseudomembranous colitis due to C. difficile infection
What do blood and pus in stool suggest?
IBD, enteroinvasive bacteria
What does blood alone in stool suggest?
Neoplasm, ischemia
What does no blood or pus in stool suggest?
IBS, laxatives, viral infection, Giardia, enterotoxigenic bacteria
Which conditions affect the terminal ileum?
Tuberculosis, Crohn’s disease, lymphoma
When are opiates (e.g., loperamide, diphenoxylate, atropine) contraindicated in diarrhea?
Fever or toxemia

Malabsorption

Where is fat absorbed?
Jejunum
Where are proteins absorbed?
Duodenum
Where are carbohydrates absorbed?
Duodenum
How is iron absorption regulated?
Duodenum and jejunum, according to body needs and mucosal regulatory system (mucosal block)
Which form of iron is more easily absorbed?
Organic iron
What causes malabsorption in the luminal phase?
Pancreatic enzyme insufficiency, impaired micelle formation
What causes malabsorption in the mucosal phase?
Lactase deficiency, celiac disease
What causes malabsorption in the transport phase?
Intestinal lymphangiectasia, Whipple’s disease
If the D-xylose test is positive, what is the next step?
Small intestinal biopsy
What biopsy findings suggest celiac disease?
Villous atrophy, mosaic-like scalloping
What biopsy findings suggest lymphoma?
Mucosal infiltration, lymphatic obstruction
What biopsy findings suggest Crohn’s disease?
Mucosal ulceration
What biopsy findings suggest tropical sprue?
Subtotal and patchy villus atrophy
What biopsy findings suggest Whipple’s disease?
Flat villi, foamy macrophages (PAS positive)
What test is used for vitamin B12 malabsorption?
Schilling test
What test is used for lactose intolerance?
Lactose-H2 breath test
What test is used for bacterial overgrowth?
14C-xylose breath test
What factors contribute to celiac disease?
Immunological, environmental, genetic
What is the best first test for suspected celiac disease?
IgA tissue transglutaminase (TTG)
What causes tropical sprue, and what are its features?
Caused by E. coli, Klebsiella; features diarrhea, megaloblastic anemia
What causes Whipple’s disease, and what are its features?
Caused by Tropheryma whippleii; features joint pain, neurologic manifestations, endocarditis
What is the treatment for Whipple’s disease?
Prolonged antibiotics (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, ceftriaxone) for 1 year

Drug-Induced Liver Disease

What liver condition is caused by thorotrast?
Tumors
What is the most important aspect of treating drug-induced liver disease?
Stop the offending drug

Acute and Chronic Liver Failure

What causes disturbed consciousness in acute liver failure?
Brain edema (improved by mannitol)
What causes disturbed consciousness in chronic liver failure?
Accumulation of metabolites
What is the landmark feature of acute liver failure?
Cerebral edema
What criteria are used for liver transplantation in acute liver failure?
King’s College criteria
How is liver injury classified based on R ratio (ALT/ULN ÷ ALP/ULN)?
Hepatocellular: R ≥5 Mixed: 2 < R < 5 Cholestatic: R ≤2
What is the antidote for acetaminophen-induced liver failure?
N-acetylcysteine (NAC)
What causes micronodular cirrhosis?
Alcohol, hemochromatosis, biliary obstruction, hepatic venous outflow obstruction
What causes macronodular cirrhosis?
Chronic viral hepatitis
What is a common cause of chronic liver failure?
Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH)
What is the normal portal venous pressure range?
5-10 mmHg
What is the portal hypertension (PHT) gradient threshold?
≥6 mmHg
What is the most common site of bleeding in PHT?
Esophagus
What is the second most common site of bleeding in PHT?
Anal canal
What is an intrahepatic presinusoidal cause of PHT?
Schistosomiasis (bilharziasis)
What is a post-hepatic cause of PHT?
Budd-Chiari syndrome
What is the most common cause of PHT?
Cirrhosis
What is the earliest symptom of portosystemic encephalopathy?
Reversal of normal sleep rhythm
What causes the sweetish, slightly fecal smell in portosystemic encephalopathy?
Mercaptans (foetor hepaticus)
What is constructional apraxia in portosystemic encephalopathy?
Inability to draw a five-pointed star
How is decreased mental function assessed in portosystemic encephalopathy?
Number connection test
Is asterixis specific for hepatic precoma?
No
What are common precipitating factors for portosystemic encephalopathy?
High dietary protein, constipation
What is the treatment for portosystemic encephalopathy?
Rifaximin (poorly absorbed, very beneficial), lactulose
What is caput medusae, and what causes it?
Collaterals radiating from the umbilicus due to engorged superficial epigastric veins
What is platypnea?
Shortness of breath relieved by lying down
What is orthodeoxia?
Fall in arterial pO2 in the upright position
What is the treatment for hepatopulmonary syndrome?
Liver transplantation
What is the treatment for portopulmonary hypertension?
Oral sildenafil (Viagra)
What is the treatment for hepatorenal syndrome?
Liver transplantation, terlipressin, IV albumin
What defines hepatorenal syndrome?
Functional renal failure, creatinine >1.5 mg/dL
What are the types of hepatorenal syndrome?
Type I: Rapid, progressive, presents with acute renal failure Type II: Common, presents with refractory ascites
What are the best indicators of liver function?
Serum albumin, prothrombin time (PTT)
What is used to assess the degree of liver fibrosis?
Transient elastography
What is the gold standard for assessing liver pathology, and why is it not used in all cases?
Liver biopsy; not used due to bleeding risk
What are indications for liver transplantation (LT)?
Acute fulminant liver failure, chronic liver disease (Child-Pugh C), hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) depending on the case
What score determines the indication for liver transplantation?
Child-Pugh score
What score prioritizes liver transplantation?
Model for End-Stage Liver Disease (MELD)
Why is MELD more accurate than Child-Pugh?
No ascites in MELD
What parameters are included in MELD?
Bilirubin, INR, creatinine
What parameters are included in the Child-Pugh score?
Encephalopathy, bilirubin, PTT, ascites, albumin, INR
What correlates with a very poor prognosis in cirrhosis?
High creatinine

Ascites

What is the most common cause of ascites?
Portal hypertension (PHT)
What causes splanchnic arterial vasodilation in PHT-related ascites?
Portal hypertension
What causes dilutional hyponatremia in ascites?
Severe water retention secondary to increased vasopressin
What is the best test to differentiate ascites due to portal hypertension vs. non-portal hypertension?
Serum-Ascites Albumin Gradient (SAAG)
What SAAG value suggests portal hypertension?
SAAG >1.1 g/dL
What SAAG value suggests non-portal hypertensive causes?
SAAG <1.1 g/dL
What is the mainstay therapy for ascites?
Diuretics
Why is spironolactone preferred over furosemide in ascites?
Spironolactone does not cause hypokalemia
What are the side effects of furosemide in ascites treatment?
Hypokalemia, hyponatremia, hypovolemia with prerenal azotemia
What is the IV albumin dose during large-volume paracentesis (LVP)?
8 g/L of ascites removed
When is large-volume paracentesis (LVP) indicated?
Grade 3 or 4 ascites, refractory to diuretics, cardiorespiratory distress due to ascites
What are complications of Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt (TIPS)?
Encephalopathy, heart failure, TIPS occlusion (give anticoagulants)
What are the clinical features of Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)?
Ascites, fever, abdominal pain, rebound tenderness
What is the diagnostic criterion for SBP on aspiration?
>250 neutrophils
What is the treatment for SBP?
Antibiotics

Metabolic Liver Disease

What is the AST:ALT ratio in Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD)?
AST:ALT <1
What is the AST:ALT ratio in Non-Alcoholic Steatohepatitis (NASH) progressing to cirrhosis?
AST:ALT >1
What limits the sensitivity of ultrasound in NAFLD?
Steatosis <20% or BMI >40 kg/m²
What is the best imaging for detecting lesser degrees of steatosis in NAFLD?
MR spectroscopy
What is the cornerstone treatment for NAFLD?
Lifestyle modification
What is the safe alcohol consumption threshold for women and men to avoid Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)?
Women: <20 g/day Men: <30 g/day
What are risk factors for ALD?
Continuous drinking, female gender, genetics (PNPLA3 gene), obesity
What lab finding may suggest alcohol misuse in ALD?
Elevated mean corpuscular volume (MCV) without anemia
What is the single most important treatment and prognostic factor in ALD?
Stop alcohol consumption
What medications are used in ALD?
Corticosteroids, pentoxifylline
What parameters are included in the Glasgow score for ALD?
Age, white cell count (WCC), bilirubin, urea, prothrombin time (PTT)
What are the clinical features of hemochromatosis?
Men >40 years, liver disease, diabetes mellitus, heart failure, testicular atrophy, chondrocalcinosis, leaden-grey skin pigmentation
What is the treatment for hemochromatosis?
Venesection, deferoxamine (Desferal), liver transplantation
What are the lab findings in Wilson’s disease?
Serum copper and ceruloplasmin reduced or normal, urinary copper increased
What is the most important clinical clue in Wilson’s disease?
Kayser-Fleischer rings
What is the treatment for Wilson’s disease with neurological manifestations?
Penicillamine
What is the treatment for Wilson’s disease without neurological manifestations?
Trientine (Syprine, safer)
When is acute fatty liver of pregnancy more common?
Twin pregnancies, first pregnancies, male fetus
What characterizes acute (cellular) rejection after liver transplantation?
Early, responds well to immunosuppressive therapy
What characterizes chronic ductopenic rejection after liver transplantation?
Occurs later, vanishing bile duct syndrome
